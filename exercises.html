Breath & Nervous System

Every session opens here. Breath is not a warm-up formality — it is the regulator that decides whether the nervous system is available for fine motor control at all. Rushed, shallow breathing keeps the system braced for effort; slow nasal breathing lets stabilizers switch on without global tension.

Diaphragmatic (360°) Breathing

Tier 0
aka belly breathing, 3D breathing
Setup & Execution
  • Lie supine, knees bent, feet flat, or sit tall cross-legged.
  • Hands on lower ribs, thumbs toward spine, fingers toward navel.
  • Inhale through the nose, expanding ribs sideways and belly forward — not just the chest lifting.
  • Exhale slowly through the mouth, feel the ribs and belly draw back down and in.
  • 4–6 second inhale, 6–8 second exhale. 8–12 breaths.
Why It's Introduced First

The diaphragm is a respiratory muscle and a core stabilizer. Before any static hold (hollow, arch, beast) can be trained honestly, you need a breathing pattern that doesn't collapse under trunk tension. This is the mechanism, not a relaxation add-on.

Develops

Rib/diaphragm dissociation from accessory neck breathing; parasympathetic downshift before skill work; baseline for bracing without breath-holding.

Common Mistakes
  • Chest-dominant breathing (shoulders rise).
  • Over-gripping the belly instead of expanding it.
  • Rushing the exhale.
Mastery Criteria

10 slow breaths, ribs expand laterally and belly forward on every inhale, shoulders stay quiet, no breath-holding to "brace."

Joint Preparation — Controlled Articular Rotations (CARs)

Borrowed directly from Functional Range Conditioning. A CAR is the slowest, most controlled possible rotation of a joint through its full available range, generated entirely by your own muscles — no momentum, no assisting hand. It is simultaneously a warm-up, an active-range assessment, and (over months) a range-of-motion training tool. If a joint is stiff or shaky somewhere in the arc, that is diagnostic information, not a flaw to rush past.

CARs — General Method (Neck, Shoulder, Hip, Spine)

Tier 0
Execution
  • Neck: seated tall, trace the largest smooth circle your chin can draw, forward-side-back-side, both directions, ~60% of max range at first.
  • Shoulder: standing, arm straight, draw the biggest circle possible from the shoulder blade, spine tall and still (no leaning to fake range), both directions.
  • Hip: standing on one leg (hold a wall lightly if needed), lift the free knee and trace the largest circle the hip can make — flexion → abduction → extension → adduction — pelvis quiet.
  • Spine: quadruped Cat–Cow, segmenting tail → lumbar → thoracic → neck, one vertebra at a time, then the reverse.
  • 1–2 slow reps per joint per direction. Speed of a clock's second hand, not a warm-up swing.
Why It's First, Every Session

You cannot safely load a range you cannot actively control. CARs establish which ranges are "owned" (strong and controlled) versus merely "visited" (loose but weak, or tight and avoided) before any locomotion or skill work asks the joint to work in that range.

Develops

Active range of motion (AROM), joint proprioception, early detection of asymmetries between left/right, a felt map of "where I am tight" vs "where I am weak."

Common Mistakes
  • Using momentum instead of muscular control.
  • Letting the spine/pelvis move to cheat range (e.g., leaning the trunk during a shoulder CAR).
  • Racing through — CARs are slow by design.
Mastery Criteria

Full circle, both directions, both sides, with no compensation elsewhere and no catching/shaking at a specific point (a "stuck point" means keep training that exact arc, don't avoid it).

Wrist Conditioning

Tier 0 → 3
progressive loading sequence, trained every session
Progression
  • Stage 1 — CARs: fingers interlaced or fist, slow max-range circles both directions.
  • Stage 2 — Prayer & reverse prayer stretch: palms together fingers up, then fingers down, gentle static holds, 20–30s.
  • Stage 3 — Quadruped rocks: hands flat on floor, fingers spread, rock weight forward/back through the wrists, knees down, slow and small at first.
  • Stage 4 — Loaded holds: quadruped weight-bearing static hold, then shifting weight side to side (feeds directly into Beast).
  • Stage 5 — Fingertip & knuckle variations: introduced once flat-hand loading is pain-free and controlled (Month 2+).
Why It Matters

Almost every ground-locomotion and inversion skill in this program (Beast, Ape, Crab, Headstand prep, Wall Handstand prep) loads the wrist in extension under body weight. Untrained wrists are the single most common reason beginners stall or get discomfort in animal-flow style training. This is trained proactively, not after something hurts.

Develops

Wrist extension tolerance under load, tissue capacity for weight-bearing, forearm/hand proprioception.

Common Mistakes
  • Jumping straight to full quadruped loading before CARs and stretch stages feel comfortable.
  • Collapsing through the wrist crease instead of spreading load across the whole hand.
Mastery Criteria

2 minutes of accumulated flat-hand weight-bearing (across sets) with no pain and stable wrist alignment → clears you to load Beast/Ape freely.

Regression
Reduce load by keeping more weight in the knees/feet, or work on fists/knuckles temporarily while extension range improves.

Ankle Conditioning

Tier 0 → 2
Progression
  • Stage 1 — Ankle CARs: seated or kneeling, lift foot, draw the largest circle from the ankle only, both directions.
  • Stage 2 — Weighted dorsiflexion rock: tall kneeling, one foot forward flat, rock knee over toes and back, heel stays down as long as possible.
  • Stage 3 — Deep-squat ankle loading: full active deep squat, gently rock knees side to side to load end-range dorsiflexion (feeds directly into the Deep Squat entry).
Why It Matters

Deep Squat, Ape, and Traveling Ape all require substantial ankle dorsiflexion. Most desk-adapted adults lose this range first and compensate by rounding the lower back or lifting the heels — ankle work removes the actual bottleneck instead of forcing a fake range elsewhere.

Develops

Ankle dorsiflexion AROM, foot/ankle proprioception, tolerance for loaded end-range.

Mastery Criteria

Knee can travel visibly past the toes in a lunge-rock with heel glued down, both sides, no pain.

Unlocks
Deep SquatApe

Core & Trunk Control

"Core strength" here means the trunk's ability to resist unwanted movement and transmit force between the upper and lower body — the still frame that lets the limbs move freely. Hollow and Arch are the two shapes almost every gymnastic and animal-locomotion skill in this program is built from.

Hollow Body Hold

Tier 1
Progression
  • Regression: supine, knees bent, feet down, lower back pressed to floor, arms reaching toward knees — "dish" without lift.
  • Base: supine, knees bent 90°, shins parallel to floor, lower back glued down, shoulders lifted slightly, arms long by ears.
  • Progression: extend one leg, then both legs, then add slow hollow rocks (small controlled rocking along the spine's curve).
Why It's Foundational

Hollow body is the shape underneath Beast Crawl, Ape, Crab, Headstand, and Wall Handstand — anywhere the spine must resist extension under load. It is introduced in week 1 because nearly every later skill borrows this exact trunk shape.

Develops

Anterior chain endurance, lumbo-pelvic control, breathing under trunk tension (this is why breath work precedes it).

Common Mistakes
  • Lower back arches off the floor (losing the shape rather than holding it).
  • Holding the breath instead of breathing through the hold.
  • Neck straining forward instead of trunk doing the work.
Mastery Criteria

30s hold, full extended-limb position, continuous breathing, lower back in contact with the floor throughout.

Progression Signs

Hold feels quiet (not shaking) at 20s+ → add leg extension, then rocking.

Unlocks
Beast CrawlHeadstand PrepWall Handstand Prep

Arch Body Hold

Tier 1
Progression
  • Base: prone, arms long overhead, lift chest and thighs slightly off the floor simultaneously, gaze toward the floor (neck neutral), squeeze glutes gently.
  • Progression: extend the hold, then add slow arch rocks (rocking gently forward-back on the belly, precursor to rolling and to Scorpion).
Why It's Foundational

Arch is hollow's mirror — posterior chain control in extension. It underlies Scorpion, back-support transitions, and rolling patterns, and balances out a program that would otherwise be flexion-dominant.

Develops

Posterior chain endurance (spinal erectors, glutes), extension control, counterbalance to hollow-dominant training.

Common Mistakes
  • Yanking the neck back to "lift higher" instead of keeping it neutral.
  • Using a big breath-hold instead of continuous breathing.
Mastery Criteria

30s hold, chest and thighs both lifted, neck neutral, continuous breath.

Unlocks
ScorpionRolling Patterns

Dead Bug & Bird-Dog

Tier 0
Execution
  • Dead Bug: supine, hips and knees at 90°, lower back flat on floor; slowly extend one arm overhead and the opposite leg straight, keeping the lower back glued down, return, alternate.
  • Bird-Dog: quadruped, extend one arm forward and the opposite leg back without letting the pelvis rotate or the low back sag.
Why It's Included

These are the bridge drills between a static hollow/arch hold and a moving limb — trunk stays still while limbs move independently. That skill (dissociation) is exactly what locomotion patterns like Beast Crawl demand.

Develops

Anti-rotation and anti-extension trunk control under moving limbs; contralateral coordination.

Mastery Criteria

8 slow reps per side, no pelvis rock, no low-back arching off the floor, breathing continuous.

Unlocks
Beast CrawlGet-Up

Ground Positions & Active Mobility

These are the "home base" shapes of the whole practice — positions you should be able to rest in, breathe in, and move out of in any direction. Movement Culture treats the deep squat and cross-legged floor sitting as baseline human positions, not stretches.

Deep Squat (Active)

Tier 1
Progression
  • Regression: heel-supported squat (small rolled towel or book under heels), hands holding a wall/door frame for counterbalance.
  • Base: feet roughly hip-width, toes slightly out, sink hips between heels, heels flat, chest lifted, elbows can hook inside knees to open hips.
  • Progression: remove hand support, then add gentle weight shifts (rocking knees side to side, shifting weight forward onto toes and back onto heels), then transitions in and out from standing without hands.
Why This Is Priority One

The deep squat is the single most-repeated shape in this entire program — it is the entry and exit point for Ape, the seated base for Shin Box, and a full-body mobility screen (ankle, hip, spine) in one position. Nearly everything else in Month 1 is scheduled around building an unassisted deep squat.

Develops

Ankle dorsiflexion, hip flexion under load, lumbar control in flexion, comfort spending time low to the ground.

Common Mistakes
  • Heels lifting off the floor (ankle range bottleneck — regress to heel support, keep training ankle CARs).
  • Lower back rounding heavily and collapsing rather than staying long.
  • Holding the breath rather than breathing while seated in the position.
Mastery Criteria

2 minutes seated in an unassisted deep squat, heels down, breathing normally, able to shift weight and lift one hand off the floor without falling back.

Progression Signs

Can hold 60s+ with hands free → begin Ape (squat-to-reach).

Unlocks
ApeShin BoxGet-Up

Shin Box / 90-90

Tier 1
Execution
  • Sit with front leg bent 90° in front of you, back leg bent 90° to the side/behind — both knees and hips near 90°.
  • Hold the static position first, tall spine, even weight on both sit-bones.
  • The switch: drive through the front foot and back knee to rotate both legs to the mirrored 90-90 on the other side, staying low and controlled, no hands.
Why It's Introduced Alongside the Squat

Shin Box trains internal and external hip rotation — the range the deep squat doesn't touch. Together, squat + shin box cover almost the full functional range the hips need for ground-based locomotion (Ape, Sit-Through, Get-Up all borrow from this rotation).

Develops

Hip internal/external rotation AROM, seated balance, the specific "unweighting and pivoting through the hips" pattern used in Sit-Through and Get-Up.

Common Mistakes
  • Using the hands to muscle the switch instead of the hips.
  • Leaning far back to create momentum rather than rotating actively.
Mastery Criteria

5 clean switches each direction, no hands, without losing tall posture.

Unlocks
Sit-ThroughGet-Up

Animal Locomotion Patterns

The Animal Flow / MovNat core of the program. These patterns load the shoulders and hips contralaterally, close to the ground, at low risk — this is where "body control under load" actually gets trained, well before any advanced skill.

Beast (Static Hold → Loaded Beast)

Tier 1 → 2
Progression
  • Base: quadruped, hands under shoulders, knees under hips, lift knees ~2–5cm off the floor, spine long (neither arched nor rounded), weight balanced between hands and toes.
  • Loaded Beast: from the hold, slowly shift weight forward onto the hands, then back toward the heels, then side to side — knees staying just off the ground throughout.
  • Beast Reach: from the loaded hold, reach one hand forward while maintaining the hold on three points.
Why Here

Beast is the base position for Beast Crawl and the entry/exit shape for Sit-Through and Scorpion. It's introduced as a static hold first so shoulder and hip stability are established before any crawling asks the limbs to move.

Develops

Shoulder stability under load (scapular control), hip flexor/extensor control, whole-body tension without stiffness, wrist loading tolerance.

Common Mistakes
  • Hips piking up (weight escaping backward) or sagging down (losing the flat back).
  • Locking the elbows instead of maintaining slight, active bend.
Mastery Criteria

30s static hold with knees hovering, flat spine, then 10 clean weight shifts in Loaded Beast without the knees touching down.

Progression Signs

Loaded Beast feels stable and unhurried → ready for Beast Crawl.

Unlocks
Beast CrawlSit-ThroughScorpion

Ape (Squat-Reach) → Traveling Ape

Tier 1 → 2
Progression
  • Base: from a deep squat, place both hands on the floor shoulder-width in front of you, then shift weight into the hands and briefly float the hips/feet, landing feet back in a squat slightly forward or back of the start. This is a "hop," not a step.
  • Traveling Ape: chain several hops in one direction, staying low the entire time, never rising to standing between reps.
Why Here

Ape is the deep squat put into motion — it demands everything the squat trained (ankle/hip range) plus the wrist loading trained separately, combined into one committed weight transfer. It is deliberately delayed until both prerequisites are solid so the movement is coordinated, not just brave.

Develops

Committed weight transfer between hands and feet, staying low through a transition, spatial/coordinative timing.

Common Mistakes
  • Rising up to standing height between hops instead of staying low ("popping up" defeats the purpose).
  • Landing heels-first instead of whole foot, or hands too far from the feet.
Mastery Criteria

5 hops in place staying low throughout; then 8+ Traveling Ape hops covering ground in one direction, controlled landings.

Unlocks
Beast Crawl

Beast Crawl

Tier 2
Execution
  • From Loaded Beast, move one hand and the opposite knee forward together (contralateral), then the other hand and opposite knee — knees staying low, hips level, no swaying.
  • Move slowly enough that you could stop and hold at any point.
  • Progress from forward-only, to backward, to lateral crawling.
Why Here (Month 2)

This is the payoff of Beast + Loaded Beast + Dead Bug/Bird-Dog: contralateral limb movement under load, low to the ground, is one of the oldest cross-body coordination patterns available and is heavily used in Animal Flow and MovNat. It's withheld until the static and weight-shift versions are solid so the crawl is smooth rather than lurching.

Develops

Contralateral coordination, sustained shoulder/hip stability under dynamic load, spatial awareness moving on all fours.

Common Mistakes
  • Hips rising higher than shoulders (reverting to a "bear crawl" rather than staying low).
  • Moving same-side limbs together instead of contralaterally.
Mastery Criteria

10 controlled contralateral steps forward and 10 backward, hips staying level with or below shoulders throughout.

Unlocks
Sit-Through

Crab (Static Hold → Crab Walk)

Tier 1 → 2
Progression
  • Base: sit, hands behind hips fingers pointing toward feet (or forward, whichever wrist angle is comfortable early on), knees bent, lift hips so trunk is roughly parallel to the floor, head neutral (don't drop it back).
  • Crab Walk: from the hold, move one hand and the opposite foot together, contralaterally, keeping the hips lifted throughout.
Why Here

Crab is Beast's posterior mirror — shoulder extension and hip extension under load, facing the opposite direction. Together, Beast and Crab give the shoulders and hips balanced loading in both flexion-dominant and extension-dominant positions, and both feed directly into Sit-Through, which is the bridge between them.

Develops

Shoulder extension strength and control, hip extension under load, posterior chain endurance, wrist tolerance in a different loading angle than Beast.

Common Mistakes
  • Letting the hips sag toward the floor between steps.
  • Dropping the head back instead of keeping the neck neutral.
Mastery Criteria

20s static hold, hips level; then 10 contralateral Crab Walk steps without the hips dipping.

Unlocks
Sit-Through

Sit-Through

Tier 2
Execution
  • From Beast, reach one hand across and forward while threading the opposite leg underneath and through to the far side, rotating the hips to end facing upward in a Crab-like position on that side.
  • Reverse the same path back to Beast.
  • Move slowly at first — this is a rotation-through-the-core drill disguised as a transition.
Why Here

Sit-Through is quite literally Beast and Crab connected — it cannot be trained honestly until both of those holds are stable, which is why it sits at the start of the "integration" work in Month 2. It's also the direct prerequisite for Scorpion.

Develops

Trunk rotation under load, hip mobility in the transition, coordinating four limbs through a changing base of support.

Common Mistakes
  • Collapsing the trailing hip to the floor instead of controlling it through.
  • Rushing the rotation instead of controlling the midpoint.
Mastery Criteria

5 controlled reps each side, Beast → Sit-Through → back to Beast, no dropped hip, even tempo both directions.

Unlocks
Scorpion

Scorpion

Tier 3
Execution
  • From prone (Arch Body base), reach one foot up and across toward the opposite hand, rotating through the spine and hip, while both hands (or forearms early on) stay grounded for support.
  • Return with control, alternate sides.
Why It's Last

Scorpion combines Arch Body's extension control with the rotational hip/spine mobility trained in Sit-Through and spinal CARs. It's the clearest "integration" skill in the program — a direct demonstration of everything trained since week 1, which is why it's reserved for Month 3.

Develops

Thoracolumbar rotation under extension load, hip mobility in a rotated/extended position, shoulder stability supporting the rotation.

Common Mistakes
  • Forcing range with a jerky kick instead of a controlled reach.
  • Letting the supporting shoulder collapse.
Mastery Criteria

5 slow, controlled reps per side, foot visibly approaching the opposite hand, no jerking, supporting arms stable.

Ground Transitions & Rolling

The ability to move between lying, sitting, kneeling, and standing fluidly — and to fall or roll without bracing rigidly — is one of the most functionally important and most neglected qualities in modern movement training. This is trained deliberately, not left to chance.

Rocking (Log Roll & Fetal Rock)

Tier 1
Execution
  • Log Roll: lie fully extended, arms overhead, roll the whole body as one unit end to end across the floor, staying long.
  • Fetal Rock: curl into a tucked ball (knees to chest, arms wrapped around shins, chin tucked) and gently rock along the length of the spine, forward and backward.
Why It's Introduced Early

These are the lowest-stakes way to reintroduce the spine to rolling on the ground and to build comfort with the head moving off-center — a direct precursor to real rolling patterns and to general ease being on the floor.

Develops

Segmental spinal control, comfort with the head/eyes moving through inverted-ish positions briefly, vestibular familiarity with rolling.

Mastery Criteria

Fetal Rock: 10 smooth rocks, chin tucked, no jerking; Log Roll: 3 full-body rolls each direction staying straight.

Unlocks
Rolling Patterns

Rolling Patterns (Shoulder Roll Progression)

Tier 2 → 3
Progression
  • Stage 1 — Rock to shoulder: from a deep squat, place one hand down, tuck chin to chest, and rock diagonally back onto the opposite shoulder blade (never the top of the head), then rock back up to the squat.
  • Stage 2 — Continuous shoulder roll: add enough momentum to continue the roll across the back diagonally and stand back up in one flowing motion, still avoiding head/neck contact.
  • Stage 3 (Month 3, optional): link the roll to a step or to Ape, treating it as a way of absorbing a fall or changing levels rather than a party trick.
Why Here

This borrows the judo/parkour "shoulder roll" rather than a gymnastics forward roll specifically because it never loads the cervical spine (unlike a traditional forward roll onto the head/neck), making it a safer entry point for adult beginners while still building the same falling/rolling competence.

Develops

Safe fall/roll mechanics, spatial orientation while inverted-ish, confidence moving the head off a fixed vertical axis, tucking coordination.

Common Mistakes
  • Rolling over the top of the head/neck instead of the diagonal shoulder-to-hip line — stop immediately and regress if this happens.
  • Untucking the chin mid-roll.
Mastery Criteria

5 continuous rolls each direction on a soft-enough surface, chin tucked throughout, standing tall at the finish, no neck loading.

Prerequisites
RockingHollow BodyArch Body

Get-Up from the Floor

Tier 2 → 3
Progression
  • Stage 1 — Half-kneeling get-up: from lying, roll to a side, come to a seated position, step one foot flat in front of you into a half-kneeling lunge, then stand — using a hand on the floor for support as needed.
  • Stage 2 — No-hands get-up: remove the supporting hand, relying on the deep squat and shin box mobility trained earlier.
  • Stage 3 — Multi-directional get-up: practice getting up leading with either side, and from lying in different starting orientations (face up, face down).
Why Here

This is MovNat's central philosophy in one movement: a real-world competency (getting off the ground under control at any age) built explicitly from prerequisite skills — deep squat, shin box rotation, and trunk control — rather than trained as an isolated drill.

Develops

Integrated whole-body sequencing, hip mobility applied functionally, confidence and options for getting up off the ground without relying on furniture.

Common Mistakes
  • Using momentum/flailing instead of a controlled, replicable sequence.
  • Always leading with the same side — practice both.
Mastery Criteria

5 clean repetitions per side without hand support, smooth and unhurried, from at least two different starting orientations.

Prerequisites
Deep SquatShin BoxDead Bug / Bird-Dog

Balance

Balance is trained as a progression of constraints (a stable base → a smaller base → removed vision → an unstable surface), not as a single exercise. Each stage is only added once the previous one is unremarkable.

Single-Leg Balance Progression

Tier 1 → 3
Progression
  • Stage 1: static single-leg stance, eyes open, 30–45s per side.
  • Stage 2: add slow free-leg movements (small circles, reaching forward/side/back — a mini "balance CAR").
  • Stage 3: close the eyes in static stance (removes visual input, forces reliance on proprioception/vestibular sense).
  • Stage 4 (Month 3): add an unstable or unpredictable element — stance on a folded towel/cushion, or a partner-free light perturbation (a soft push, self-initiated hop-to-stick).
Why It's Trained This Way

Balance is proprioception made visible. Progressing the constraint (rather than just "trying harder") is how Movement Culture and gymnastics coaches actually build it — each stage removes a crutch the nervous system was relying on.

Develops

Proprioceptive acuity, ankle/hip micro-stabilization strategies, calm response to instability (relevant directly to Headstand and Wall Handstand work).

Common Mistakes
  • Gripping the floor with the toes instead of distributing pressure across the whole foot.
  • Holding the breath while balancing.
Mastery Criteria (per stage)

45s stable, unhurried hold before advancing to the next stage; regress immediately if a stage causes bracing/holding the breath rather than calm control.

Inversion & Handstand Preparation

Inversion work is introduced last and progresses conservatively — wall-supported throughout this program. The goal for these 12 weeks is comfort, alignment, and time-under-tension upside down, not a freestanding handstand (that is a multi-month goal beyond this program; see each Month document's "what's next").

Headstand Preparation

Tier 2
Progression
  • Stage 1 — Tripod setup drill: kneeling, place forearms and hands on the floor forming a stable triangle (hands + head width apart), practice placing the crown of the head on the floor just in front of the hands without any weight on it yet.
  • Stage 2 — Weight shift: from the tripod, gently rock weight forward onto the head/forearms and back to the knees, feeling how much control is needed, hips staying low.
  • Stage 3 — Wall-supported tuck: facing away from a wall at head-distance, walk the feet up to a tucked position with hips over the tripod base, wall as a light backstop only, not a crutch to fall into.
Why Here

By Month 2, Hollow Body and wrist/CARs work give you the trunk control and wrist tolerance to explore inversion safely; this is deliberately scaled far below a full headstand attempt.

Develops

Comfort being inverted, neck-safe weight distribution (mostly forearms/hands, minimal on the head), proprioception upside down.

Common Mistakes
  • Loading weight onto the top of the head instead of the hand/forearm triangle.
  • Kicking up explosively instead of a slow, controlled weight shift.
Mastery Criteria

Comfortable 15–20s in the wall-supported tuck, most of the weight on hands/forearms, calm breathing, controlled entry and exit.

Prerequisites
Hollow BodyWrist Conditioning
Unlocks
Headstand Hold

Headstand Hold

Tier 3
Execution
  • From the wall-supported tuck, slowly extend the hips and legs upward toward the wall, aiming for a long line from elbows to feet, weight predominantly on the hand/forearm triangle.
  • Breathe normally; descend with the same control used to enter.
Why It's Last

Straightening the whole body upside down demands the hollow-body control, wrist/forearm tolerance, and inversion comfort built across the entire program — it is the Month 3 payoff of the inversion track, not a starting point.

Develops

Full-body inverted line control, continued wrist/forearm loading tolerance, calm nervous-system state while inverted.

Common Mistakes
  • Overarching the lower back instead of keeping the hollow/long line.
  • Descending in a rush instead of with the same control as the ascent.
Mastery Criteria

20–30s controlled hold against the wall, long straight line, controlled entry and exit, calm breathing.

Wall Handstand Preparation

Tier 2 → 3
Progression
  • Stage 1 — Chest-to-wall walk-up: from a downward-dog-like position facing the wall with feet close to it, walk feet up the wall while walking hands back, stopping well within comfort (hips well below full extension at first).
  • Stage 2 — Extend the hold: gradually increase how far up the feet walk and how long the position is held, always stopping short of strain.
  • Stage 3 (Month 3): brief holds closer to full extension, hollow body actively maintained, still fully wall-supported.
Why Here

This is the direct precursor to any future freestanding handstand work and reuses the exact hollow-body shape and wrist tolerance trained since Month 1 — introduced only once both are solid.

Develops

Shoulder overhead stability under load, hollow body control while inverted, comfort and orientation upside down against a wall.

Common Mistakes
  • Overarching the back ("banana" shape) instead of holding a hollow line.
  • Walking too high too soon — stay well within a controlled range.
Mastery Criteria

20s chest-to-wall hold with an actively controlled hollow shape and steady breathing.

Prerequisites
Hollow BodyWrist ConditioningHeadstand Prep

Hanging Preparation (optional — needs a bar/branch)

Tier 3 · Optional
Execution
  • If a pull-up bar, sturdy branch, or playground bar is available: passive hang, relaxed shoulders, feet lightly touching the floor or fully off if comfortable, 10–20s.
  • Progress to active hang (shoulders engaged, "protecting" the shoulder blades rather than fully passive) for the same duration.
Why It's Marked Optional

The program's core constraint is no equipment. This entry exists because shoulder-hanging is a valuable complement to the ground-based work here and the user's own list requested it — but it is explicitly a bonus branch, not a gate to anything else in this program.

Develops

Shoulder decompression and passive range, grip tolerance, scapular control under a hanging load.

Mastery Criteria

20s passive hang and 15s active hang, relaxed breathing, no shoulder pinching pain.
