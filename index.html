Philosophy

This program is built the way a Movement Culture coach would build one: not around exercises, but around qualities — the raw physical and perceptual capacities that any skill is eventually made of. A handstand is not a "goal" here; it's a late expression of hollow-body control, wrist tolerance, and inverted balance, all trained separately, years or months before the shape itself matters.

Adaptability over performance

The target is a body that can improvise a solution to a novel movement problem — not a body that can hit a number. Sessions end with unstructured exploration for exactly this reason.

Quality over quantity

A 15-second Hollow Body hold with continuous breathing and a flat lower back is worth more than a 40-second hold that's shaking and breath-held. Every dosage in this program assumes you'll choose quality over the written number when they conflict.

Body intelligence over metrics

Proprioception — knowing where your body is without looking — is trained as deliberately as strength. Balance progressions, slow CARs, and exploration time exist specifically to build this sense, which no rep count can capture.

Nothing here is measured in calories, muscle size, or "intensity." The only enemies in this practice are rushing and forcing — moving faster than your control allows, or muscling through a range you haven't actually earned.

The Assessment System

The same battery repeats at four checkpoints: before Week 1 (baseline), end of Month 1, end of Month 2, and end of Month 3 (final). Each Month document contains the exact battery for that checkpoint plus a gate — a short list of criteria that indicate you're ready for the next tier rather than just "having done" the weeks.

CheckpointWhereCore purpose
BaselineMonth 1, before Week 1Honest starting point — nothing to compare against yet, just a record
End of Month 1Month 1, Week 4Confirms Tier 1 statics are solid enough to load dynamically in Month 2
End of Month 2Month 2, Week 8Confirms locomotion patterns are fluent enough to combine in Month 3
FinalMonth 3, Week 12Full-system snapshot — direction of change across all four checkpoints matters more than any single number

Gate criteria are intentionally forgiving (roughly 75% of listed criteria, not 100%) — the point is to catch a genuinely under-built foundation before it's loaded further, not to gatekeep perfectionistically. When in doubt, an extra week on the same content is always the safer choice than pushing ahead.

Glossary

Terms used throughout all four documents, defined once here.

Mobility
The combination of range of motion and the control to use that range under your own strength — distinct from flexibility, which only describes passive range.
Active Range of Motion (AROM)
How far a joint can move under its own muscular effort, with no external assistance. This is what CARs train and measure.
Passive Range of Motion (PROM)
How far a joint can move with external help (a hand, gravity, a wall). A large gap between PROM and AROM at a joint is a specific, trainable weakness, not just "tightness."
Stability
The capacity to control or resist unwanted motion at a joint — most of this program's "core" work is trunk stability: keeping the spine still while limbs move.
Proprioception
The sense of where your body parts are in space, without looking. Trained explicitly through CARs, balance progressions, and eyes-closed work.
Interoception
The sense of internal bodily state — breath, heart rate, tension, fatigue. Breathing practice and cool-downs train this directly.
Coordination
The nervous system's ability to sequence and time multiple body parts together toward one outcome — what Beast Crawl and Get-Up are fundamentally training.
Locomotion
Moving the whole body across the ground under its own power — Beast Crawl, Ape, Crab Walk, and Sit-Through are all locomotion patterns.
Controlled Articular Rotations (CARs)
The slowest possible, fully self-generated rotation of a joint through its available range — see the Exercise Library's Joint Preparation section for full method.
PAILs / RAILs
Progressive/Regressive Angular Isometric Loading — an FRC end-range strength method, one layer beyond CARs. Referenced in Month 3's "Beyond Week 12" as a next step, not trained directly in this program.
Compensation pattern
A substitute movement the body uses to fake a range or strength it doesn't actually have (e.g., arching the low back to appear to squat deeper). Named explicitly under "Common Mistakes" for every skill in the Library.
Contralateral / Ipsilateral
Contralateral = opposite-side limbs moving together (right hand, left knee) — the pattern in Beast Crawl and Crab Walk. Ipsilateral = same-side limbs together.
Closed chain / Open chain
Closed chain: the limb's end (hand or foot) is fixed against the ground (Beast, Crab, Deep Squat). Open chain: the limb moves freely in space (a CAR, a kick). This program is closed-chain-dominant, which is generally lower-risk for beginners.
Isometric / Concentric / Eccentric
Isometric: holding still under tension (Hollow Body hold). Concentric: muscle shortening under load (standing up from a squat). Eccentric: muscle lengthening under load (lowering into the squat with control).
End-range strength
Strength expressed at the very outer limit of a joint's range, rather than in the middle. The reason CARs precede any loaded skill — you don't want strength only in the "safe middle" of a range.
Deload
A deliberately reduced-intensity period (this program's consolidation weeks — 4, 8, 12 — function as light deloads) that lets adaptation catch up to training.
Flow state / Flow (movement)
Both a psychological state of absorbed, effortless focus, and (in Animal Flow terminology) a self-composed sequence linking multiple patterns — Month 3, Week 11 trains both meanings at once.
Motor learning
How the nervous system acquires a new movement pattern, typically through three stages: cognitive (effortful, clumsy), associative (refining), and autonomous (automatic). Referenced in Month 2 when Beast Crawl and Ape feel clumsy at first.

Movement Journal & Tracking

Treat this less like a workout log and more like a lab notebook — the point is to capture qualitative signal (what felt smooth, what felt stuck) alongside the few numbers that matter. A plain text file or spreadsheet works fine; the structure below matters more than the tool.

Daily entry (after each session)

FieldWhat to capture
Date & session (A/B/C)Which rotation slot, so you can see cycle frequency over time
DurationActual time trained, not planned
Movement quality (1–5)Your own rating of how controlled the session felt — not effort, not soreness
What felt smoothOne or two specific movements — this builds a record of real progress that raw hold-times miss
What felt stuckWhere you noticed shaking, breath-holding, or a compensation — this is where next session's attention goes
Pain / stop signalsAny sharp pain, joint pinching, or numbness — log it even if minor, and regress that movement next time
One exploration noteAnything noticed during the unstructured exploration block — these notes often predict next month's "aha" moments

Weekly review (end of each week)

Skim the week's daily entries and answer in a few sentences: what pattern showed up more than once (a recurring stuck point, a recurring breakthrough)? Is any single joint or shape consistently the bottleneck? Should next week's dosage change from what's written, based on how the body actually responded — not on what "should" happen next per the plan?

Monthly assessment log

Keep one running table across all four checkpoints (Baseline, End M1, End M2, Final) with the battery items as rows and checkpoints as columns — this is the one piece of the journal worth formatting as an actual table or spreadsheet, since the whole point is reading numbers across a row.

General Resources

Month-specific reading is listed at the end of each Month document. These are broader, program-wide references worth exploring anytime.

  • Ido Portal — Movement CultureLong-form talks and interviews on training movement as a generalist practice rather than a sport — the closest single influence on this program's overall philosophy.
  • Animal Flow (Mike Fitch)The source of the Beast/Ape/Crab/Scorpion/Sit-Through vocabulary used throughout this program.
  • Functional Range Conditioning (Dr. Andreo Spina)The source of CARs and the active/passive range of motion distinction used throughout the glossary and mistakes sections.
  • GMB FitnessStructured bodyweight-gymnastics progressions (wrist prep, elbow levers, handstand theory) — a useful complement once you're comfortable with this program's pacing.
  • MovNat (Erwan Le Corre)The source of the Get-Up's "no wasted movement, real-world competency" framing, and the natural-terrain branch mentioned in Month 3's "Beyond Week 12."
